The Mythic Scale of Steinbeck’s Environmental Fiction | An Earth Systems Capstone | Erin Pang |

Abstract

Steinbeck’s novels are often noted for depicting the relationships between people and their environments and the ways in which human and environmental systems are interconnected. When environmental crises occur in his stories, they arise because humans have lost this sense of scale and have ceased to understand themselves as part of an interconnected system. But dealing with an issue of such vast scale poses challenges. How does one show that the invisibly small (from the nutrients in soil to phytoplankton in the ocean) is connected to the incomprehensibly large (from the ocean tides to the Earth itself)? Steinbeck’s solution is to use mythic elements to appeal to our primitive relationship with the natural world, evoking an intuitive understanding of the complexity of global systems. Faced with today’s environmental crisis, it is worthwhile to revisit Steinbeck’s works as environmental literature that can teach us how to communicate the importance of living in accordance with the environmental systems that sustain us.

At the time that Steinbeck was writing, environmental degradation, at least in the modern context of climate change, had not yet been identified as a foremost challenge facing humanity, and thus Steinbeck’s works were not originally read as environmental literature. However, their recognition of humans’ impact on the environment were advanced for their time, and if literature is meant to interact dynamically with readers and their circumstances, then now may be the appropriate time to ask what Steinbeck’s striking vision of humans and the environment can tell us about our current environmental challenges . . .
